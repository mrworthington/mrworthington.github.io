In America, You Have No Right To An Education

How Texas’ school finance system helped perpetuate nationwide inequality.

This essay is the first in a series about how school finance drives  inequality.

Forty-four years ago, the United States Supreme Court ruled that children have no fundamental right to receive an education. If you never knew that, you aren't alone—people are surprised when they discover this little-known piece of American history born out of San Antonio’s West Side.

Roe v. Wade got all the headlines in 1973, but just three months after that landmark decision, the Supreme Court reached another landmark 5-4 decision: San Antonio Independent School District v. Rodriguez, in which a group of San Antonio families from the Edgewood community lost their years-long fight for equal education for their children. The situation has been re-litigated multiple times, but it remains the reason that extreme inequalities persist in American schools today.

The case’s roots trace back to May 16, 1968, when 400 Edgewood High School students walked out to protest antiquated curricula, abysmal facilities, and consistently poor funding. In reponse, Mexican-American families from Edgewood hired lawyer Arthur Gochman and sued Texas for discrimination in a federal court. They claimed that Texas school funding methods—which are linked to property taxes—were responsible for Edgewood’s inferior quality as a district, and that Edgewood was powerless to change their circumstances.

Texas responded by claiming it provided “a minimum” education, which was all they were legally required to do. Gochman used that term to frame what was at stake if the court ruled in favor of “minimums” and against the Edgewood families. America, he said, risked endorsing a dual-class society where some have access to real opportunity and others do not.

Gochman argued passionately on behalf of poor children living in the most resource-deprived school districts of San Antonio. And while the case was underway,  many observers expected the plaintiff to prevail. Governors and other state legislators were bracing for what Justice Powell later deemed “an unprecedented upheaval in public education.” As the New York Times reported at the time: “Every state has studies under way, and many have passed or are considering legislation to eliminate” the fundamental inequalities in the U.S. system of school finance. .”

Yet, the moment never came. On March 21, 1973, the court dispassionately ruled against Rodriguez and the 14 other Mexican American families from Edgewood. Instead, they found that because education is not explicitly enumerated in the constitution as a right, it could not be protected under the 14th amendment’s Equal Protection clause.

The court claimed that it highly valued education. In the majority opinion written by Justice Powell, the court reaffirmed their contention from Brown v. Board of Education that “education is perhaps the most important function of state and local governments.” The court also expressed belief in the unjust character of America’s education system. They never reasoned that it was fair or sensible.

Yet, they  believed it was constitutional.  In supporting the majority decision against Rodriguez, Justice Potter Stewart wrote: “The method of financing public schools in Texas, as in almost every other State, has resulted in a system of public education that can fairly be described as chaotic and unjust.  It does not follow, however, and I cannot find, that this system violates the Constitution of the United States.”

In just two sentences, the court acknowledged called America’s public education system “chaotic” and “unjust,” while also affirming that it was “constitutional.”

In his dissent, Justice Marshall mourned the court’s decision and wrote prophetically of America’s bleak future in a post-Rodriguez world where education was not a fundamental right:

“ I, for one, am unsatisfied with the hope of an ultimate ‘political’ solution sometime in the indefinite future while, in the meantime, countless children unjustifiably receive inferior educations that ‘may affect their hearts and minds in a way unlikely ever to be undone.’”

Since that ruling in 1973, no United States citizen has the fundamental right to an education. Your children don't have that right. Your neighbor's children don't have that right. You don't have that right. You could be a descendant of George Washington [a][b][c]or Abraham Lincoln, and you still would not have that right to an education in America.

Americans have some fundamental rights that aren't explicitly written into our constitution. For instance, the right to procreate and the right to vote in a state election are granted to everyone and are protected by the constitution. Yet, no one has the fundamental right to be educated. This court case—San  Antonio ISD v. Rodriguez—is responsible for ensuring that the right cannot be granted. Four decades later, America’s poor are still plagued by a broken and unjust education system. 

Since 1973, we have tried many things to fix education. We have tried fixing standards, teachers, school leaders, students, parents, and everything in between. Two fixes we’ve never called for: Amending the constitution to protect the right to an education, and transforming school finance systems that courts since Rodriguez have continued to condemn. They call it “unjust,” “byzantine,” and “awful,” but they have yet to call it unconstitutional.
